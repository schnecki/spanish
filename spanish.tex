\section{Subjects and Verbs}


The key in learning an new language is to understand how to use the verbs. Therefore, we concentrate
on these. There exist two tracks of verbs in Spanish, those verbs ending in
\textbf{-ar}\refer{tracks of verbs} and those ending in \textbf{-er} or \textbf{-ir}. For instance,
we have \textsf{habl\textbf{ar}}\VerbS{hablar}{to speak} for \textsf{to speak}, and
\textsf{com\textbf{er}}\VerbS{comer}{to eat} for \textsf{to eat} and
\textsf{viv\textbf{ir}}\VerbS{vivir}{to live} meaning \textsf{to live}\footnote{also see
  \url{https://www.spanishdict.com/guide/spanish-present-tense-forms}}.

\paragraph{Pronunciation.} The pronunciation is easy in Spanish as words are pronounced as they are
written but there can only \textbf{one single stress} in each word. If there is an apostrophe the
corresponding vowel/syllable is stressed, otherwise the second to last syllable is stressed. For
instance, we have \textbf{hab-lar}, thus \textbf{hab} is stressed. In Spanish the leading \textbf{h}
is not pronounced, thus hablar is pronounced as \textbf{ah}-blahr, with the bold part being
stressed. All vowel sounds, even unstressed, have to come out clearly and always pronounced the
same: a, e like in let, i, o like in obey, u like in food.
%
Furthermore, the \textbf{v} is pronounced as \textbf{b}, and \textbf{ll} as \textbf{j}.

\paragraph{Present Tense Conjungation.}
To conjugate -ar verbs, remove the -ar ending and add the corresponding ending of the desired
subject. For instance with the word \Verb{hablar}{to speak} we get the top part in
Table~\ref{tbl:conj}.

Note that \textsf{el} is masculine, \textsf{ella} is feminine and \textsf{usted} is formal for you.
In case of group of peoples we have \textsf{ellos} for groups of man or mixed groups, \textsf{ellas}
for groups consisting only of woman, and \textsf{ustedes} for taking formally to a group. For -er
endings, remove the -er ending and add the corresponding subject ending. We use \Verb{comer}{to eat}
as example, cf.~Table~\ref{tbl:conj}.

And very similar to the -er verbs, are the -ir verbs, where only the nosotros and vosotrs endings
are different. For \Verb{vivir}{to live} is presented in the bottom part of Table~\ref{tbl:conj}.

\section{Special, Stem-Changing and Irregular Verbs}


\paragraph{Special Verbs.} First of all there of are a handful special verbs that do not follow the
regular pattern in the \textsf{yo} form, though they are easy to remember as they are very common.
This of course also means that they important to know. These are:

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \begin{tabular}{llll}
    \toprule
    Infinitive & Meaning         & yo form             & Meaning   \\
    \midrule
    decir      & to say/tell     & yo di\textbf{g}o    & I tell    \\
    hacer      & to do           & yo ha\textbf{g}o    & I do      \\
    poner      & to put          & yo pon\textbf{g}o   & I put     \\
    salir      & to go out/leave & yo sal\textbf{g}o   & I go out  \\
    suponer    & to suppose      & yo supon\textbf{g}o & I suppose \\
    tener      & to have         & yo ten\textbf{g}o   & I have    \\
    traer      & to bring        & yo trai\textbf{g}o  & I bring   \\
    venir      & to come         & yo ven\textbf{g}o   & I come    \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \caption{Irregular verbs.\VerbS{hacer}{to do}}
\end{table}

We call these words the \textbf{go-go words}\refer{go-go words} as the end in -go. Additionally to
these words other words ending in -poner fall into the same category. There is one more word that is
different, but not a go-go word: \Verb{haber}{to have}. As \textsf{tener} it also means \textsf{to
  have}, but it is used when talking about the past. We will investigate this in detail later.

\paragraph{Spelling Changes.} Some verbs do not follow the rules of the previous section, i.e.\@
there is a spelling change in the yo form
\footnote{\url{https://www.spanishdict.com/guide/spanish-present-tense-forms}}.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Verbs ending in -guir the yo form ends in go.} The \textsf{yo} form ends in
  \textsf{go}, e.g.\@ \Verb{extinguir}{to extinguish} is \textsf{yo
    extingo}.
\item \textbf{Verbs ending in -ger or -gir, the g in the yo form changes to a j.} For instance
  \Verb{direct}{to direct} we get `yo dirijo' for `I direct'.
\end{itemize}

Here are some common verbs that use these two rules:

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering

    \toprule
    Infinitive & Meaning       & yo form                                 \\
    \midrule
    extinguir  & to extinguish & extingo \VerbS{extinguir}{to extinguish} \\
    dirigir    & to direct     & dirijo \VerbS{dirigir}{to direct}        \\
    escoger    & to choose     & escojo \VerbS{escoger}{to choose}        \\
    exigir     & to demand     & exijo \VerbS{exigir}{to demand}          \\
    recoger    & to pick up    & recojo \VerbS{recoger}{to pick up}       \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \caption{\label{tbl:guir} Verbs with -guir, -ger and -gir ending have different yo forms.}

\section{Tenses}

There exist present, past and future tenses in Spanish:

\subsection{El tiempo presente (The Present Tense)}

The present tense is signaled by stressing the second to last syllable. This stress signals the
present tense and the ears tune in on the ending to find out the subject that is talked about.

\subsubsection{Simple Present Tense.} That is actually what we have been using all the time up to this
point. You can use it to talk about
\begin{enumerate}
\item Habitual Actions: %
  \Phrase{Me levanto a las seis y media cada mañana.}{I get up at six thirty every morning.} %
\item Things happening now: %
  \Phrase{¿Cómo estas?}{How are you?} %

  \Phrase{Limpio la cocina.}{I clean the kitchen. (I am cleaning the kitchen.)} %
  %
  \AdverbS{Cómo}{How} %
  \AdverbS{Qué}{What} %
  \AdverbS{bien}{fine/well/okay} %
  \NounS{la cocina}{the kitchen} %
  \NounS{gracias}{thank you} %
  \VerbS{hacer}{to do} %
  \VerbS{estar}{to be} %
  \VerbS{to clean}{limpiar} %

  \Phrase{Uno más uno son dos.}{One plus one is two.} %

  %

  attached to the infinitive.

\section{Other Rules}

The word for \textsf{and} in Spanish is \textsf{y}, but if the consecutive word starts with
\textsf{i} or \textsf{hi} and sounds like \textsf{y}, then \textsf{e} is used instead. Por
ejemplo\PhraseS{por ejemplo}{for example}:\textsf{ \(\ldots\), e irán mejorando los tratamientos
  paliativos} means \textsf{ \(\ldots\), and palliative treatments will improve}. Otro ejemplo es:
\textsf{español e ingles}.
